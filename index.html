Short answer: Snowflake isn't a competitor. It's a data source. Snowflake Intelligence answers questions about the data inside Snowflake. Milo gives you a company 360 view by joining Snowflake to everything else — your spreadsheets, your tool data, other databases, the operational systems that don't and shouldn't live in the warehouse. Six axes follow, ordered by how much each one matters for Blue Diamond specifically.

Coverage, not competition

Snowflake Intelligence covers Snowflake. Milo gives you a company 360 view.

Snowflake Intelligence (Cortex Analyst, Cortex Search, Snowflake Copilot) is a credible NL-to-SQL layer over your Snowflake data. We've benchmarked it carefully. The six axes below aren't flaws in Cortex. They're the things that come from Cortex's scope being one source, while Milo's is the whole estate.

01 / Coverage

Cortex sees Snowflake. Milo joins everything.

Cortex Analyst answers questions whose data lives inside Snowflake. Useful, but most operational signal at Blue Diamond does not.

SAP CAR till data, WooCommerce orders, Stripe payments, MailChimp campaigns, Trustpilot reviews, weather APIs, footfall counters, supplier feeds. Milo joins across them in one conversation, with Snowflake as one source among many, not the only one.

For Blue Diamond: the question "did the rainy weekend in Surrey hurt plant sales at Cobham more than at Coolings?" needs SAP CAR + a weather API + footfall. Two of three are not in Snowflake.
02 / Maintenance burden

Cortex needs YAML. Milo bootstraps itself.

Cortex Analyst requires hand-built semantic models in YAML for every domain you want to query, maintained as schemas evolve. For a lean data function, that maintenance debt compounds fast.

Milo bootstraps semantic context from existing dbt models, Cube/LookML if present, or directly from the warehouse. New tables surface automatically. The data team approves them rather than authoring them.

For Blue Diamond: with a small data team, Milo's auto-context is the realistic option. Cortex Analyst's YAML maintenance is a part-time job nobody has the capacity to take on.
03 / Surface

Cortex is an API. Milo ships the surface.

Cortex Analyst is a query API. Snowsight is its default surface, and yes, you can embed Cortex elsewhere. But you'd be building the chat UI, the auth, the multi-system join layer, the Teams/Slack adapter, the citation rendering, and the audit trail yourself.

Milo ships all of that out of the box. Centre Managers ask the question in the channel they're already in. The answer arrives next to today's emails, not in a tool you'd have to first build then ask them to open.

For Blue Diamond: Centre Managers across the estate aren't going to become Snowsight users, and you don't want to build the surface yourself. They're already in Teams. Meet them there.
04 / Cost shape

Cortex bills per query. Milo is platform-priced.

Cortex Analyst consumes Snowflake credits per question, with cost a function of compute time and tokens. With unpredictable casual usage, monthly spend is volatile and grows with adoption.

Milo is platform-priced per active user. A Centre Manager asking ten questions a day costs the same as one. Adoption stops being a finance risk.

For Blue Diamond: the goal is more questions asked, not fewer. A pricing model that makes finance nervous about more questions is the wrong shape.
05 / Multi-turn context

Cortex is one-shot. Milo is a conversation.

Cortex Analyst is largely single-turn. Each question is independent. Follow-ups need to re-establish context.

Milo holds conversational state across turns and across data domains. "And now compare that to last year" works. So does "drill into Cobham, then show me the staffing on those days, then check the cafe attach rate."

For Blue Diamond: real questions are not one-shot. They are an investigation, three or four queries deep, often crossing domains.
06 / Action, not just answer

Cortex stops at SQL. Milo can act.

Cortex Analyst returns rows. Useful, but the next move is manual. Open another tool, send the message, raise the ticket.

Milo can post the answer back to a Slack channel, draft an email to the Regional Manager, raise a Jira ticket against a stock issue, or write back to a Google Sheet. Permissioned, audited, optional.

For Blue Diamond: the stockout alert is more useful as a draft message to the buyer than as a row in a table.
The other tool you've been pitched

And yes, we know about ThoughtSpot. Briefly.

ThoughtSpot is a destination tool. Users go to ThoughtSpot, search, get a chart. The semantic layer is Worksheets, modelled and curated up front. Excellent product, well-architected, deserves the credit it gets.

Two practical differences for Blue Diamond. First, surface. Milo's bet is that the analytics destination model is what gets disrupted next. Centre Managers will not adopt a new tool. A real Blue Diamond question

Three queries Cortex Analyst can't answer alone. Milo can.

Each example below requires a join across systems where at least one is not in Snowflake. Each is the kind of question a Centre Manager or the Trading team actually asks on a Monday.

01 / Weather × footfall × till

"Did the rain hurt Le Friquet more than Trentham last weekend?"

Three sources. SAP CAR has the till. The Met Office has the rainfall. Footfall counters at the door have the visit count. Snowflake has none of them in one place today.

Milo joins them by centre and date, returns weather-adjusted variance, and tells you whether Le Friquet's drop is real or just wet.

What Milo is not

The honest list. What we don't replace.

An SA brief that pretends the new tool replaces everything is a brief you correctly throw away. So here is the honest list.

Doesn't replace

Your warehouse.

Snowflake stays your source of truth. Milo reads it, never replaces it. If anything, deeper warehouse adoption follows from more questions being asked.

Doesn't replace

Your dashboards.

Daily monitoring is still a dashboard job. Milo is for the question that doesn't fit a dashboard, asked once, in passing, in chat.

Doesn't replace

Your data scientist.

Advanced modelling, forecast tuning, statistical work — that's still a human. Milo handles the high-volume operational questions, freeing them for the harder ones.

Does replace

Three meetings a week.

The "can someone pull this for me" Slack messages, the Friday afternoon ad-hoc reports, the Monday trade-meeting reconciliation. Those collapse into a chat thread.
